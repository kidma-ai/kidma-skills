# Kidma Lesson Plans — Deep Dive Analysis ("Secret Sauce")

> **Based on:** Full content analysis of 5+ lesson plan documents from תשפ"ו across all program types (Advanced A, Advanced B, Entrepreneurship Workshop). This document extracts the recurring patterns, structures, and pedagogical principles that make Kidma lessons work.

---

## 1. Two Distinct Document Formats

Kidma uses **two different lesson plan formats** depending on the program type:

### Format A: Full Instructor Guide (מערך שיעור למדריך)
Used for: **Advanced A** and some **Advanced B** lessons
- Written **for the instructor**, not the student
- Contains the full teaching script, not just tasks
- Includes instructor tips, classroom management notes, timing
- Has rich theoretical content the instructor explains verbally
- Follows a strict 4-phase structure (see below)

### Format B: Task Sheet (דף הנחיות / הנחיות לעבודה)
Used for: **Entrepreneurship Workshop**, some **Advanced B** lessons
- Written **for the student** directly
- Contains numbered missions/tasks to complete
- Each task specifies: instructions, AI tool type, and expected output
- Less teaching script, more self-directed
- Students work with AI tools following structured prompts

---

## 2. The Core 4-Phase Structure (Format A)

Every Format A lesson follows this exact flow, always totaling **90 minutes**:

### Phase 1: הקדמה (Introduction) — ~5 minutes
- Instructor introduces themselves (first lesson) or recaps previous lesson
- Sets expectations and previews today's topic
- Often includes an **ice-breaker question** connected to the lesson output
- Example: "What's your favorite main dish and dessert?" (leads into recipe creation task)

### Phase 2: הבנה (Understanding) — ~20 minutes
- **Theory delivery** broken into digestible chunks
- Follows a pattern of: **Concept → Analogy → Examples → Visual**
- Uses age-appropriate metaphors (puzzles, LEGO, friends, cooking)
- Introduces **technical vocabulary** with kid-friendly explanations
- Often includes a **short video** or **interactive demo**
- Key principle: *"Break the main idea into small parts that are easy to understand"*

### Phase 3: התנסות (Experimentation) — ~20 minutes
- Guided hands-on activity with AI tools
- **Everyone does the same task** first (unlike creation which is personal)
- The task is designed to demonstrate a specific concept
- Often includes **comparison exercises** (English vs Hebrew prompts, different outputs from same prompt)
- Purpose: *"Direct experience to truly understand the core idea"*

### Phase 4: יצירה (Creation) — ~35 minutes (the biggest block!)
- Students create a **personal output** using what they learned
- Always starts with instructor explaining the task with screens closed
- Follows a sub-structure:
  1. **Task definition** (הגדרת המשימה) — what to create
  2. **Deepening requirements** (העמקה בדרישה) — specific constraints
  3. **Brainstorming** (סיעור מוחות) — peer collaboration encouraged
  4. **Output** (תוצר) — submit via Kidma Portal
- Key principle: *"Translating understanding into innovation and personal expression"*

### Closing: סיכום (Summary) — ~5 minutes
- Recap key points learned
- Ask for questions
- **Verify all students submitted** their outputs

## 3. The Three Learning Objectives Framework

Every lesson defines objectives in three categories (always in this order):

### הבנה (Understanding)
What concepts/knowledge students will gain. Written as "Students will understand/know..."
- Always 3-6 bullet points
- Covers both the AI concept AND the domain knowledge

### התנסות (Experimentation)
What hands-on practice students will do. Written as "Students will experience..."
- Always 2-4 bullet points
- Focuses on the process, not the output

### יצירה (Creation)
What personal output students will produce. Written as "Students will create..."
- Always 1-2 bullet points
- Specifies the exact deliverable

---

## 4. Lesson Plan Components Checklist

Every Format A lesson plan contains these sections (in order):

1. **Title** (שם השיעור) — topic name + subtitle
2. **Lesson Objectives** (מטרות השיעור) — the 3-part framework above
3. **Materials & Preparation** (עזרים והכנה לשיעור) — tools, portal setup, pre-lesson testing
4. **Learning Materials** (חומרים ללמידה) — YouTube links, articles for instructor self-study
5. **Lesson Structure** (מבנה השיעור) — the 4-phase flow with timings
6. **Assessment** (הערכה) — how to measure lesson success (3-4 metrics)

## 5. Recurring Pedagogical Principles

### 5.1 "The Prompt is King" (הפרומפט הוא המלך)
This mantra appears in multiple lessons. Students are taught that the quality of their output is directly tied to the quality of their prompt.

### 5.2 Start from the End
Students are taught to think about the desired OUTPUT first, then work backwards to craft the right prompt. "What result do I want? → What role should the AI play? → What constraints apply?"

### 5.3 Role-Based Prompting
Across all lessons, students learn to assign roles to AI: "You are a chef", "You are a photographer", "You are a poet". Each role brings its own vocabulary.

### 5.4 The Domain Vocabulary Principle
Each AI domain (text, image, music, code) has its own professional vocabulary:
- **Text:** role, tone, structure, length, style
- **Image:** lighting, colors, angles, focus, background, color palette, texture
- **Music:** tempo, genre, instruments, mood
- **Code:** functionality, design, responsiveness

### 5.5 Hebrew → English → AI → Hebrew Pipeline
Due to AI tools working better in English, Kidma teaches a consistent workflow:
1. Think/plan in Hebrew
2. Translate to English (using Google Translate)
3. Submit prompt in English to AI tool
4. Translate output back to Hebrew if needed

### 5.6 Iteration is Expected
Students are explicitly told: "You won't get the perfect result on your first try." The lesson plans emphasize iterating on prompts, refining, and improving.

### 5.7 Process Over Product
Assessment evaluates the JOURNEY (prompt evolution, thinking process) not just the final output.

### 5.8 Peer Collaboration
Every creation phase includes explicit encouragement for students to help each other, share prompts, and brainstorm together. "Your collaborative work creates a power greater than the individual."

### 5.9 Paper First, Screen Second
For image-generation lessons especially: students must first sketch/write their idea on paper, then translate to a prompt. No jumping straight to the AI tool.

## 6. Format B Pattern (Task Sheets)

The Entrepreneurship and some Advanced B lessons use a different structure:

### Each Task Contains:
1. **Task title** and number
2. **Task type** (learning / research / brainstorming / writing)
3. **Numbered instructions** (5-6 steps, each starting with "Ask the AI to...")
4. **AI tool type** specified (e.g., "text-to-text chatbot")
5. **Expected output** description

### The Task Progression:
Tasks always progress from **learning → research → brainstorming → creation → synthesis**

### Final Submission Task:
Always includes a consolidation task where students compile all previous work into a single polished document.

---

## 7. Assessment Metrics Pattern

Every lesson defines 3-4 simple success metrics:
1. Students **understood** the key concepts (comprehension check)
2. Students **experimented** with the tool (hands-on verification)
3. Students **created** outputs (deliverable check)
4. Students **submitted** in the portal (administrative check)
5. (Sometimes) Students **enjoyed** the lesson (engagement check)

---

## 8. Content Depth Pattern

Each lesson includes two layers of content:

### Surface Layer (for students):
- Simple analogies and metaphors
- Age-appropriate language
- Visual examples
- 1-2 sentence explanations

### Depth Layer (for instructor):
- Technical details in smaller font or separate sections
- "Learning materials" links for self-study
- Notes like "for instructor deepening, no need to cover in class at this stage"
- Behind-the-scenes technical explanations

---

## 9. Language & Tone

- Written entirely in **Hebrew**
- Uses **second person plural** ("התלמידים ילמדו..." / "הדריכו את התלמידים...")
- Warm, encouraging tone
- Frequently uses **bold** for key terms and concepts
- Uses **metaphors from everyday life** (cooking, puzzles, LEGO, friends, travel)
- Technical terms always appear in both Hebrew and English

---

## 10. Tool Integration Pattern

Every lesson specifies:
1. **Which AI tools** will be used (by name)
2. **Which mode** (query-response vs chatbot)
3. **Portal configuration** needed (which tools enabled, approval settings)
4. **Instructor pre-testing** requirements ("test the tool before class!")
5. **Fallback options** (if a tool is blocked on school network)

---

## Summary: The Recipe for a Kidma Lesson

To generate a new Kidma lesson, you need:

1. **Pick a topic** with an AI angle (what AI capability does this teach?)
2. **Define the output** (what personal thing will students create?)
3. **Write 3-part objectives** (understand / experiment / create)
4. **Build the 90-minute flow:**
   - 5 min intro with hook question
   - 20 min theory with analogies + demo
   - 20 min guided experimentation (same task for all)
   - 35 min personal creation (individual outputs)
   - 5 min summary + submission check
5. **Include:** domain vocabulary, role-based prompting, iteration emphasis, peer collaboration, paper-first approach
6. **Specify:** tools, portal setup, instructor prep, assessment metrics
7. **Write in Hebrew** with English technical terms, warm tone, everyday metaphors
